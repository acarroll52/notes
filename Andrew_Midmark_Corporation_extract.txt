1) What is blood pressure?
Blood pressure is the force that circulating blood exerts on the walls of blood vessels. It’s reported as 
two numbers in millimeters of mercury (mmHg):
Systolic BP (SBP) — the top number. Pressure when the heart contracts and ejects blood.
•
Diastolic BP (DBP) — the bottom number. Pressure when the heart relaxes between beats.
•
A related value: Mean arterial pressure (MAP) — an average pressure driving blood through the 
circulation. Approximate formula:
MAP ≈DBP + 1/3 × (SBP −DBP)
2) How BP is measured — two main methods
A. Auscultatory (manual) method — sphygmomanometer + 
stethoscope
You place a cuff around the upper arm (over the brachial artery), inflate it to stop blood flow, then 
slowly deflate while listening with a stethoscope at the antecubital fossa.
•
Korotkoff sounds:
Phase I: first clear tapping — systolic pressure.
○
Phase V: disappearance of sounds — diastolic pressure (used in adults).
(Phase IV is muffling — sometimes used in special populations.)
○
•
B. Oscillometric (automated) devices
Most automatic BP monitors detect oscillations in the cuff pressure produced by arterial pulse 
during deflation. Many calculate mean arterial pressure from the oscillation amplitude and 
algorithms, then estimate systolic and diastolic from that.
•
Advantages: easy, less operator-dependent. Limitations: can be less accurate with arrhythmias, 
very low or very high pressures, or improper cuff size.
•
3) Preparing the patient (keys to accuracy)
Before measuring, make sure:
Patient has rested quietly for 5 minutes (no exercise immediately prior).
•
No caffeine, smoking, or heavy meal for 30 minutes if possible.
•
Seated with back supported, feet flat on floor, legs uncrossed.
•
Arm supported at heart level (on table), palm up.
•
Remove tight clothing over the arm — cuff directly on bare skin.
•
Use an appropriately sized cuff (see next section).
•
If possible, avoid talking during measurement.
•
4) Cuff size & placement
Cuff bladder width ≈40% of mid-arm circumference.
•
Cuff bladder length ≈80% of arm circumference.
•
If cuff is too small → false high readings. If too large → false low readings.
•
Place the cuff so bladder center lies over the brachial artery, ~2–3 cm above the antecubital fossa.
•
5) Step-by-step: Manual (auscultatory) BP measurement

Use a validated device for clinical use (many consumer devices are not validated).
•
Use correct cuff size and arm position at heart level.
•
Take 2–3 automated readings 1–2 minutes apart and average them.
•
Be cautious with arrhythmias — automated devices may be inaccurate.
•
7) Interpreting BP (commonly used thresholds)
Guidelines vary by organization. A commonly used classification (2017 ACC/AHA) is:
Normal: < 120 / < 80 mmHg
•
Elevated: 120–129 / < 80 mmHg
•
Hypertension — Stage 1: 130–139 or 80–89 mmHg
•
Hypertension — Stage 2: ≥140 or ≥90 mmHg
•
Home / daytime ambulatory thresholds: average daytime ABPM or average home BP ≥135/85 mmHg
is often considered hypertensive (again, thresholds differ by guideline).
Important: diagnose hypertension on the basis of multiple readings taken on different days, or with 
ambulatory/home monitoring when needed.
8) Accuracy problems / common sources of error
Things that raise readings:
Cuff too small, arm below heart level, patient talking, anxiety (“white coat”), recent 
caffeine/smoking/exercise, full bladder.
•
Things that lower readings:
Cuff too large, arm above heart level, deflating too quickly, observer bias.
•
Other issues:
Arrhythmias (AF, PVCs) cause unreliable oscillometric and auscultatory readings. Use manual 
palpation and consider multiple readings, or an auscultatory technique with experienced observer.
•
Measuring over clothing, or with the arm unsupported, increases error.
•
9) Special situations
Orthostatic (postural) BP: measure supine (after 5 min), then standing at 1 and 3 minutes. A drop
in SBP ≥20 mmHg or DBP ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing indicates orthostatic 
hypotension.
•
White coat hypertension: elevated office BP but normal out-of-office readings. Consider home or 
ambulatory monitoring.
•
Masked hypertension: normal office BP but elevated out-of-office BP; also detected with 
ambulatory/home monitoring.
•
Children: cuff size and BP norms are age/height dependent — use pediatric tables.
•
Pregnancy: special considerations (e.g., preeclampsia assessment) — thresholds and follow-up

1) The basic physical idea
When the cuff is inflated above systolic pressure it occludes the artery and there are no pulse-related 
pressure oscillations in the cuff. As the cuff pressure falls below systolic, pulsatile blood flow returns and 
small pressure pulses inside the cuff begin to appear. These cuff pressure pulses increase in amplitude 
as cuff pressure approaches the arterial mean pressure, reach a maximum at MAP, then decrease as the 
cuff pressure falls further (because the transmural pressure changes and the arterial wall dynamics 
change). The device measures the cuff pressure and the superimposed oscillations and uses that 
relationship to estimate MAP, SBP, and DBP.
2) Hardware and raw signal
Cuff + bladder transmits arterial pulsations to a pressure transducer.
•
Analog front end: pressure transducer → amplifier → anti-alias filter → ADC.
•
Sampling rates are usually in the tens to low hundreds of Hz (enough to capture heartbeats and 
motion artifacts).
•
The device collects two things in real time: absolute cuff pressure (slowly changing DC 
component) and AC component = small oscillations.
•
3) Signal processing pipeline (typical)
Acquire raw cuff pressure Pcuff(t)P_{cuff}(t)Pcuff(t). This contains a slowly varying baseline 
Pbase(t)P_{base}(t)Pbase(t) plus pulsatile oscillations posc(t)p_{osc}(t)posc(t).
1.
Detrend / remove baseline — subtract a low-frequency estimate so only pulsatile content 
remains (helps remove deflation slope).
2.
Bandpass filter the pulsatile signal to keep the cardiac frequency band (typical band ≈0.5–10 Hz; 
exact edges depend on device). This removes drift and very fast noise.
3.
Rectify / envelope detection — take absolute value and low-pass filter or compute peak-to-peak 
per beat to form an oscillation-amplitude time series. A common approach: detect beat peaks and 
measure peak amplitude (or compute peak-to-trough). Then associate that amplitude with the 
cuff pressure at that time.
4.
Produce the oscillation envelope: amplitude vs cuff pressure. Interpolate to get a smooth 
envelope (e.g., spline).
5.
Find the maximum amplitude on the envelope → pressure at that maximum = MAP (by 
convention).
6.
Estimate SBP and DBP: find cuff pressures on either side of the MAP where the envelope crosses 
empirically determined fractions of the maximum amplitude (these fractions are device-
dependent). Example: if Amax is the max amplitude, find pressure where amplitude rises to R_s × 
Amax on the inflation/ascending side = SBP; find pressure where amplitude falls to R_d × Amax on 
the deflation/descending side = DBP. Some algorithms use more advanced regression models 
rather than single fixed ratios.
7.
Quality checks: check beat-to-beat variability, SNR, motion artifacts, too few pulses, arrhythmic 
beats; flag or discard and repeat if poor quality.
8.

SBP and DBP estimation: classic oscillometric algorithms use fixed amplitude ratios:
SBP = cuff pressure where envelope rises to R_s × Amax on the ascending side (before 
MAP), and
○
DBP = cuff pressure where envelope falls to R_d × Amax on the descending side (after MAP).
○
•
Typical ratios reported in the literature and device docs vary: R_s commonly ≈0.5–0.7, R_d 
commonly ≈0.7–0.9. There is no universal standard — many manufacturers tune these via 
clinical calibration studies (and some use models or look-up tables that depend on heart rate, age, 
arterial stiffness, etc.).
•
Because SBP/DBP require these empirical mappings, different devices can systematically differ vs 
auscultatory readings.
•
5) Important signal/algorithm details & variations
Deflation vs inflation mode: Most devices measure during deflation (continuous slow deflation). 
Some measure during inflation (faster, sometimes more comfortable). Algorithms must account 
for different dynamics.
•
Deflation rate: recommended ≈2–3 mmHg/sec for auscultation; many oscillometric devices also 
use similar rates or step/hold methods. Too fast deflation yields too few samples; too slow is 
uncomfortable and prone to drift.
•
Peak detection: must be robust to missing beats, ectopics, and variable pulse amplitude. Many 
implementations use beat templates and cross-correlation or adaptive thresholding.
•
Envelope smoothing & interpolation: spline or polynomial fits give smoother envelope for 
threshold crossing calculation.
•
Artifact rejection: motion, talking, tremor produce large, noncardiac oscillations. Devices detect 
sudden irregular peaks, inconsistent RR intervals, or amplitude outliers to reject those beats.
•
Beat averaging: many devices average amplitudes from several consecutive beats at each 
pressure level to reduce beat-to-beat variability.
•
Adaptive algorithms: modern devices may adjust the SBP/DBP thresholds depending on patient 
age, heart rate, waveform shape or arterial stiffness indices.
•
6) Practical and clinical limitations
Arrhythmias (AF, frequent PVCs): beat-to-beat amplitude is irregular → envelope is noisy and 
SBP/DBP estimates can be inaccurate. Many devices either flag the measurement as unreliable or 
attempt robust averaging / outlier rejection (but accuracy remains limited).
•
Low perfusion (cold extremities, shock): oscillation amplitudes are small → poor SNR and 
increased error.
•
Motion artefact: patient movement is a major cause of error.
•
Arterial stiffness / atherosclerosis / elderly: waveform shape changes; amplitude envelope may 
be flattened or shifted, causing systematic bias. Oscillometric devices can overestimate SBP or 
underestimate DBP in stiff arteries.
•
Cuff misfit (too small / too large / wrong placement) still affects oscillometric devices the same 
way as auscultatory.
•
Obesity: cuff fit and effective bladder dimensions change readings.
•
Pediatrics & neonates: small-amplitude pulses and need special cuffs — many oscillometric 
devices are not validated for infants.
•
Calibration & validation: device accuracy depends on clinical validation (AAMI/ESH/ISO 
protocols). Unvalidated consumer devices can be quite inaccurate.
•
7) Clinical comparison to auscultation
Oscillometric devices directly estimate MAP (the strongest signal) — this is why MAP estimates 
are often reasonably accurate across devices.
•

SBP and DBP are indirect estimates and vary with the algorithm, so agreement with auscultation 
varies. In practice oscillometric SBP tends to be slightly higher and DBP slightly lower in some 
devices, but this isn’t universal. Therefore validated devices are essential for clinical use.
•
8) Validation and standards
Clinical accuracy is judged by standards such as AAMI/ANSI, European Society of Hypertension (ESH)
protocols, and ISO 81060-2 (device validation for non-invasive sphygmomanometers). Always choose a 
device that has passed a recognized validation standard for the population you’ll be measuring.
9) Practical tips for best oscillometric readings
Use a validated device (check published validation studies).
•
Use the correct cuff size, arm at heart level, seated, rested.
•
Avoid motion and talking.
•
For arrhythmias, try multiple measurements and consider manual/auscultatory check if possible.
•
If readings are unexpectedly abnormal, remeasure after repositioning and confirming cuff fit; use 
ambulatory/home monitoring if needed.
•
10) Advanced / research topics (brief)
Model-based estimation: rather than fixed ratios, some devices use machine-learning or 
parametric models that estimate SBP/DBP from entire waveform features (pulse shape, slope, 
harmonics).
•
Pulse transit time (PTT) and cuffless methods: research into estimating BP from PTT and PPG 
(plethysmoggraphic, ie SPO2). These are not yet standard replacements for cuff methods clinically.
•
Arterial stiffness indices: features of the oscillation waveform relate to arterial compliance and 
can be extracted for research/diagnostics.

Below I unpack each block in detail.
1) Cuff and tubing (mechanical)
Cuff bladder: inflatable rubber or PVC bladder sized for adult/pediatric arms (bladder width ≈40% 
arm circumference, length ≈80%). Multi-size cuffs are supplied.
•
Outer cuff shell: fabric/frame to hold bladder, Velcro closure, printed sizing.
•
Tubing & connectors: flexible hose (often 3–6 mm ID) with quick-connect fittings to console. 
Longer tubing increases damping and delays; many designs keep tubing short.
•
Hardware features: cuff pressure port, pressure-relief strap, reinforced seams; connectors may be 
keyed for pediatric vs adult.
•
Design notes: Cuff size and bladder geometry critically affect accuracy — device IFU specifies 
recommended cuffs and warnings for wrong size.
2) Pneumatic subsystem
This is the “air” side that inflates/deflates the cuff.
a. Pump (inflation)
Types: miniature diaphragm (membrane) pump, piston pump, or micro-blower.
•
Requirements: must inflate cuff to target pressure (often up to 250–300 mmHg) within a few 
seconds (e.g., 3–10 s). Typical pump flow is a few hundred mL/min to several L/min depending on 
cuff volume.
•
Implementation: small DC motor with diaphragm/plenum or piezo/brushless micro pumps. For 
continuous finger-cuff or CNAP systems pumps must be faster and more responsive.
•
b. Valves (deflation/control)
Solenoid valve (on/off): common and low cost. Deflation rate is controlled by pulsed operation 
(PWM) or duty cycle control.
•
Proportional valve: provides continuous, smoothly variable orifice for precise deflation control 
(preferred for high-precision/continuous systems).
•
Check valves / one-way valves: prevent backflow into pump and protect components.
•
Pressure relief / over-pressure valve: mechanical safety to open above a set pressure.
•
c. Reservoir / plenum (optional)
Small internal volume to smooth pump output and reduce pulsation.
•
d. Tubing routing & quick release
Quick-disconnect with automatic shutoff is common to prevent sudden loss of cuff pressure.
•
Design notes: Deflation must be controlled (≈2–3 mmHg/s for standard measurements) or performed in 
discrete steps for step-deflation devices. Proportional valves give best control and reduce oscillatory 
artifact; solenoids are cheaper.

3) Pressure sensing
a. Pressure transducer(s):
Type: piezoresistive (silicon), piezoelectric (less common), or capacitive MEMS gauge. 
Piezoresistive MEMS is typical.
•
Range: 0–300 mmHg typical; some devices spec 0–400 mmHg.
•
Accuracy: clinical devices aim for ±2–3 mmHg accuracy (or better) and low nonlinearity.
•
Resolution: device target is typically 1 mmHg readout resolution; ADC resolution and analog gain 
must support that (see AFE).
•
Placement: usually in console near pump. Some designs integrate transducer near cuff connector 
for reduced tubing effects.
•
b. Sensor considerations
Temperature drift: need temperature compensation or digital calibration.
•
Linearity & hysteresis: good sensors have small hysteresis and predictable linearity.
•
Response time: should be fast enough to capture oscillations (bandwidth up to ~10 Hz or more).
•
c. Differential vs absolute pressure
Devices usually measure gauge pressure relative to ambient. Some AFE implementations measure 
absolute and subtract ambient or use differential sensors.
•
4) Optional physiological sensors (commonly integrated)
PPG / SpO₂ sensor (finger probe): LEDs (red & infrared), photodiode, transimpedance amplifier, 
ambient light cancellation. Used for pulse rate and plethysmogram (for PTT, artifact detection, 
fusion).
•
ECG leads (3-lead commonly): for R-peak timing (PAT) or arrhythmia detection. Requires patient 
isolation circuitry and right-leg drive.
•
Accelerometer / motion sensor: detect motion to reject corrupted beats.
•
Microphone / phonocardiogram: sometimes used to detect heart sound (S1/S2) or valve opening.
•
Tonometry/pressure sensor (for specialized devices).
•
Design notes: If implementing ECG/PPG the AFE and electrical isolation (IEC 60601) requirements 
become stricter.
5) Analog front-end (AFE) & data acquisition
a. Pressure AFE
Instrumentation amplifier or sensor bridge excitation circuit for piezoresistive sensors.
•
Low-drift op amps for sensor excitation and conditioning.
•
Anti-aliasing filter (analog low-pass) before ADC.
•
ADC: 12–24 bit depending on design. For pressure oscillation detection, a 12–16 bit ADC sampled 
at 50–200 Hz is common. Higher resolution helps with small oscillation amplitudes.
•
b. PPG AFE
Transimpedance amplifier (TIA) to convert photodiode current to voltage.
•
Programmable LED drive currents, synchronous detection (LED multiplexing) to reject ambient 
light.
•
Bandpass / filter (0.5–8 Hz typical).
•
ADC: 12–16 bit, sampling 100–500+ Hz (higher if you want precise PTT timing).
•
c. ECG AFE
Instrumentation amplifier, right-leg drive, high common-mode rejection, notch filter for mains 
(50/60 Hz), sampling 250–1000 Hz.
•
Isolated inputs and patient protection circuits.
•
d. Microcontroller / ADC considerations
MCU with DMA, timers, and sufficient CPU for DSP (filtering, envelope detection). Some designs 
use a separate DSP or Cortex-M family SoC.

6) Controller & firmware
Microcontroller / SoC: runs the measurement sequence (inflate/deflate), reads ADCs, runs 
oscillometric algorithms (detrend, bandpass, envelope, MAP/SBP/DBP derivation), handles UI and 
communications.
•
Real-time control loop: controls pump/valve to achieve target pressure and deflation profile. 
Closed-loop pressure control (from pressure sensor) is typical.
•
Memory: flash for firmware, RAM for data buffers; nonvolatile storage for logs and patient 
reports.
•
Real-time clock: timestamp measurements for EMR transfer.
•
7) Power, battery & isolation
AC mains adaptor and internal power supplies (SMPS). Isolation and leakage current limits per IEC 
60601-1 (medical safety).
•
Battery: Li-ion pack + battery management system (charging IC, fuel gauge) for portable devices; 
battery must meet safety standards.
•
Isolation: patient-connected circuitry must be electrically safe (BF applied part). Isolation barriers 
between mains and patient circuits are required.
•
Surge & EMI protection: design to meet EMC requirements (IEC 60601-1-2).
•
8) Example parts / component types (for an engineer)
Pressure sensor: MEMS piezoresistive gauge 0–300 mmHg (gauge).
•
Pump: miniature diaphragm pump (DC) with ~1–5 L/min peak.
•
Valve: 2-port solenoid valve or proportional microvalve (pneumatics).
•
MCU: ARM Cortex-M4/M7 or a small SoC with DSP for real-time filtering.
•
ADC: 12–24 bit SAR or delta-sigma depending on sensor and bandwidth.
•
Op-amps: low-noise, low-drift instrumentation op amps for sensor conditioning.
•
LED/PD: For PPG, high-efficiency IR and red LEDs and a PIN photodiode or APD.
•
Accelerometer: 3-axis MEMS for motion detection.

===== PAGE 81 =====
What does this location enable?
I know Blue Ash was a strategic location choice, because this area is a hub for medical device 
companies. J&J Medtech, Mammotome, Hillrom, Atricure, and others are all co-located close by. 
That strategy has already bared fruit as myself and more than half my team recently joined from 
J&J Medtech just up the street where we designed and launched many complex surgical devices 
supporting a multi-billion dollar business.
-
How is Cor 1.0 going?
The Versailles team inherited Cor 1.0 last summer and had to work through a number of gaps in 
requirements, documentation, manufacturability, and testing. When I joined in January, I was 
genuinely impressed with both the product and the progress the team had made. Since then, 
we’ve grown the team and added even more testing and engineering rigor, which has helped us 
find and fix smaller issues ahead of launch. Cor 1.0 is a solid product, and overall it’s in a very good 
place. FDA deficiencies. At this point, we’re focused on ironing out the remaining wrinkles and 
getting it launch ready.
-
What's the status of Cor 2.0?
My team is currently very focused on launching Cor 1.0 on schedule, however, we have been 
deliberately carving out time to reduce risks on Cor 2.0. Right now, that work is centered on 
understanding customer and market needs and advancing two technical feasibility workstreams in 
partnership with Jennifer Serres. The first is the Riva blood pressure technology we acquired 
earlier this year, which I believe Dr. Tom will speak to later today. The second is wireless location 
tracking. Last month, we hired Rick Schuman, a senior engineer with deep wireless product 
development experience. Rick is leading early research with potential suppliers and building test 
plans to evaluate options, with the goal of establishing a baseline technical approach and 
confirming feasibility ahead of Gate 0 later this year.
-
What will the lab enable?
Historically, Midmark has outsourced much of the design and testing work for diagnostics 
products, which has led to inefficiencies, poor communication, and ultimately schedule slips and 
cost overruns. We are changing that. This lab enables us to bring core hardware and firmware 
development in-house here in Cincinnati, including electrical design, prototyping, and test. It gives 
us the ability to test prototypes and early builds ourselves, which increases confidence in the 
design and helps us identify issues earlier in the development process. It also improves 
collaboration with our cross-functional partners, because other teams can get hands-on with 
prototypes quickly and provide feedback much earlier.

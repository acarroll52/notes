# Tone Profile

This profile is based on sent email patterns from the last 30 days of Sent Items.

## Core Style

- concise
- direct
- practical
- clear asks
- low fluff
- polite without sounding overly formal

## What The Voice Sounds Like

- Starts with the answer quickly.
- Uses short paragraphs.
- Often keeps internal replies very brief.
- Gives concrete next steps instead of abstract framing.
- Corrects misunderstandings plainly and without drama.
- Sounds like a busy operator, not a polished marketing writer.

## Internal Email Pattern

Common characteristics:

- brief to very brief
- direct scheduling or action language
- comfortable with one-line replies
- minimal greeting if the thread already has momentum

Examples of the pattern:

- direct scheduling response
- quick confirmation
- short correction plus next step
- operational ask with a due-date implication

## External Email Pattern

Common characteristics:

- short greeting
- straightforward explanation
- concise ask or requested next step
- friendly but not chatty closing

The voice stays efficient even with vendors or outside collaborators.

## Technical Email Pattern

When describing a problem or issue:

- state the current behavior
- mention what changed or when it started
- note what has already been tried
- identify the likely question, root cause, or requested help

Avoid over-explaining unless the technical situation requires it.

## Formatting Preferences

- prefer short paragraphs
- use bullets when listing concrete items, options, or questions
- avoid giant dense paragraphs
- avoid formal memo language

## Preferred Closings

Typical closings include:

- `Thanks!`
- `Thank you,`
- name only when needed

Do not force a closing on every internal reply if the draft is meant to be short.

## Do

- be crisp
- be clear about what is needed
- make the next step obvious
- preserve factual precision
- sound human and a little busy

## Avoid

- overly polished corporate language
- long warm-up sentences
- generic empathy filler
- vague asks
- unnecessary repetition
- AI-sounding transitions

## Calibration Notes

If the user is replying to:

- a coworker: bias toward brevity
- a vendor or external partner: keep a bit more framing
- a technical misunderstanding: correct directly, then move to action
- a scheduling thread: keep it very short

Ignore signature noise and mobile footer text when learning style.

## Context Priority

When drafting a reply, prefer to ground the content in:

1. the target email thread
2. recent related sent and received email
3. relevant project notes
4. recent relevant Teams messages

Use notes and Teams to recover facts, decisions, or commitments. Use sent mail primarily to match tone and phrasing.

## Review Marker

When a reply needs missing information or a decision from the user, insert:

- `[@Andrew Carroll - <actual instruction>]`

Put the real instruction inside the brackets. Keep it short and specific.

#### Onboarding Notes
- Project number: 21062.
- Midmark 800 Digital Vital Signs Device notes: manual entry for weight, height, BMI, RR, and pain; measures BP, pulse rate, SpO2, and temperature; battery or cord powered; rechargeable Li-ion battery; two stacked USB-C ports; two Ethernet ports; multiple wall mounts; side connections for Masimo SET rainbow and likely blood pressure.
- Cart notes: no electronics except passthrough USB-C power for the vital signs device.
- Model notes: four models; one without SpO2; one with Masimo and one with Nellcor SpO2; one with Masimo SpO2 plus automated respiration rate.
- Testing notes: lifecycle testing focused on NIBP; 7-year life target with about 63k readings over 2 months; check functionality and drift after life testing; partially automated; about 30% complete as of 2026-01-06; sample size of 3.
- Compliance testing notes: most testing can be done onsite, including chemical, environmental, and leakage current; EMC is usually done at Intertek Lexington but was done in Irvine for this project.
- PCB assembly/test notes: ATE1 is the automated functional bed-of-nails tester; there is a separate NIBP tester.
- Electronics ownership notes: Midmark owns the main board, connector board, and NIBP board; the SpO2 board is licensed from Masimo or Nellcor.
- Major onboarding issues: 510(k) deficiency #11 asked for a clinical study for blood-pressure-inferred pulse rate on NIBP; incorrect threaded inserts were used in the latest build; screen flicker occurs after software update and currently requires a full power cycle; 8 kV air discharge causes touch latency; NIBP leak failures were occurring during PCB assembly/test; pulse rate could display even when BP was bad; the requirement/IFU says audio should play when connecting or sending to EMR but that was not implemented.

### 2026-02-04
#### Summative Usability Findings
- First meeting after study completion noted that there were no potentially hazardous situations, but most users committed at least one foreseeable misuse and some of those needed to be added to the foreseeable misuse list.
- Manual versus automatic measurements should follow clinician intent.
- Typing a manual entry should prevent automatic measurement from overriding it.
- When the SpO2 probe is removed and placed back on, RRp should not override a manually entered RR.
- Pressing Save should not clear all vitals, even though that was happening.
- UI motion, micro-interactions, and click states were a common source of confusion and were considered severity 2.
- RRP persistence showed previous-patient RRP for about 2 minutes until new capture; severity 3.
- RRP reading was not obvious to users, and the low-signal-quality notification appeared every time; severity 3.
- RRP crash caused the device to reset while obtaining RRP; severity 4.
- Open questions remained around manual versus automatic RR/RRp priority, override behavior, and user notification.
- Height entry was confusing: feet-to-inches conversion, decimal precision, metric-to-imperial handling, and backspace behavior were all pain points; severity 3.
- SpO2 capture had a persistent yellow highlight box when the sensor was removed; severity 3.
- Pain score UX was acceptable for now but could be improved later with better handling of pain faces; severity 2.
- Floor scale issue affected Fairbanks but not Detecto; severity 2.
- Battery icon lacked a charging state.

### 2026-03-30
#### FDA Response to Child Cuff / 510(k) Response
- Options on the table for the child cuff issue were: remove support for child cuff completely, implement an algorithm for child cuff detection, remove NIBP pulse rate from linear mode, or collect more child data.
- Existing algorithm could not distinguish between small-adult and child cuffs.
- Removing NIBP pulse rate from linear mode would affect adult patients too.
- For deficiency 2b, FDA seemed aligned and wanted redlines of changed documents.
- For deficiency 2d, updates were largely acceptable but still needed traceability back to software requirements.
- For deficiency 2a, intent may not have translated; FDA prefers a 3-tier residual-risk acceptability matrix, though a rationale for retaining 2-tier may still be acceptable.
- For deficiency 11, recommendation remained to have ECG ground truth; FDA had concerns about the data provided; 10% acceptance seemed high; irregular rhythms not being included was generally expected; single-lead ECG was sufficient; both original and new datasets should be submitted; success criteria should be tightened in the new protocol; high-level feedback might be available if the protocol is attached to meeting minutes.
- For deficiency 12, reports submitted seemed aligned, but FDA wanted assurance that Midmark software/hardware does not negatively affect OEM measurements; Medtronic had not provided test data; a summary in meeting minutes might prompt an FDA response.
- Clinical study timeline noted: submit report next week, allow 10 days for approval, a few days to get participants, and 2 to 3 weeks to test.
- The working target was mid-March with work through Friday, March 6, and a 510(k) approval target of April 15.
- IFU updates were needed beyond the FDA deficiencies, including UI/UX and other items.
- Strategy for the IFU was to create a branch containing only the 510(k) updates and submit that draft as part of the 510(k) response.
- Dr. Tom was concerned about the BP pulse-rate accuracy stated in the IFU; the source of the current 5% statement was unclear, and 10% might be closer to reality if FDA accepts the existing data.
- SRS lacked hardware details; Nick Ross was assigned to add hardware requirements and protocols.
